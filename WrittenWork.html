1.1 Describing the purpose of IT in modern business.

A comparison between early technology and modern technology is mind boggling. As a civilisation, we went from the humble typewriter to the desktop computer within the span of less than half a century. Another two decades later, both of those machines are superseded ten-fold by a device that can fit into your pocket.

So, what does this have to do with business? Modern business has also undergone similar radical changes to their model. Technology allows a supplier in Asia to communicate with their client in New York in real time, something which would have been almost impossible a century ago. Advancements in technology gives business development teams to make informed decisions based on historical data. Complex algorithms can build models and predict future behaviour of financial markets. Internal business strategies can be efficiently saved and stored with immense amounts of security, preventing such information from being leaked to a competitor.

It’s undeniable that IT is not only an essential component of advancing a modern business, but it’s also something that’s here to stay for the foreseeable future.


1.2 Describing methods, skills, and resources needed to complete digital projects successfully.

What do you need to make projects successful?

Enabled by modern technology, collaboration across different people and cultures has never been more prevalent. No matter if we are working on a small hobby project or we are trying to tie in people all over the glove for a corporation, there are some things every project needs in order to achieve success.

On that note, the first would be to have a clear definition of success. Outlining at what point the project becomes successful helps prevent the project from being delayed or over complicated.

Once success has been outlined, the right team and mindset is really important, especially with management as they are governing the direction of the project. Management of the project will need to be strong to prevent resources being spent on a demand which does not contribute to the overall success criteria.

Additionally, the right technical team is required who are both able and have enough backing and resources to complete the task at hand by the set deadline.

Lastly, a common killer of projects is beginning far too quickly and not noticing any of the possible problems. Identifying pitfalls before the project is underway can save a significant amount of time in the long run. These pitfalls then need to be appropriately communicated to all relevant team members involved in the project so it can be dealt with efficiently and also quickly authorised by senior members of staff to ensure the solution’s rapid deployment.

What Tools?

When it comes to tools, the first thing which comes to mind is hardware and software. The costs for these can often spiral out of control if purchased without a clear plan in mind. Mitigating these costs involve checking if the project can succeed on current machines, the impact using current machines will have on scalability and future-proofing, and whether or not there is a suitable alternative. In addition to the technical components, there also needs to be methods in place for team members to flag any bugs and have a clear log of discussions which can be easily continued or edited as solutions may require time to investigate. Both of these need to be cost-effective and good value as well as user friendly.

What Skills?

Skills are somewhat of an intangible element to a project although there are always certain recurring traits in a team which lead to success. Ultimately, being a good communicator is an essential skill across all levels of a project and business. Being unable to effectively present ideas, thoughts, or even concerns is something which can lead to a breakdown between internal and external customers. Leadership is also required to ensure the project stays on track and has sufficient backing to avoid any delays or setbacks.

Section 1.4 Describe the risks that might impact digital projects.

What causes digital projects to fail?

Digital projects, like all projects, are at risk of failing due to several factors. A lack of resources in the form of either cashflow or personnel can often stifle or even prevent progress of a project as the deadline looms.

A lack of direction in management can be catastrophic in terms of time management as there's a potential for the project to be more reactionary instead of proactive, making the team deal with problems as they come which can often require lengthy solutions. This impacts the project with delays, huge oversights being identified too late into the project, and having to overcome these issues whilst at a significant time disadvantage.

This leads into the next point of a project failing as there are often times a point in a project where success can no longer be achieved. Instead of discontinuing the project, more time and resources are poured into something which is no longer viable. It is therefore important to know when a project can no longer be delivered.


1.5 Describe how you would go about selecting and using IT systems and software.

How do you select the right kind of technology for the job?

Picking the right kind of technology is really important as the right decisions early on pay for themselves the further the project goes. That being said, this does not mean the best solutions are always costly.

The first question to answer would be if the task at hand can be achieved using existing technology already available to the company. This massively reduces costs and removes the significant barrier of budget.

Should it arise additional technology is required, it’s always good to research the market for potential candidates. Not only should the product be something which is a great fit for the task, compatibility with existing systems and cost should also factor in to the selection criteria.

Once products have been shortlisted, contacting the vendor for more information and a live demonstration is key to ensuring the product is suitable to the task. It’s also a great time to explore features of the product and build rapport with the vendor in the event of repeat business.

Lastly, once all the products have been subject to a live test, it’s time to re-evaluate their suitability, in particular using feedback from the team members who will be directly using the product so an informed decision can be made and a strong case made for the purchasing of new technology.

In the particular case of the website, I already have VSCode installed and some familiarity with it. Additionally, I'll be using a mixture of HTML and CSS to build the front end, with possibly some Python for the backend.

1.7 Describe legal guidelines and constraints that impact digital projects.

How does GDPR impact digital projects?

GDPR has essentially changed the way in which digital products can be run and now an essential point which must be discussed before the project is underway. As "personal data" is such a broad term, if the product involves end users data, it's almost certainly going to involve GDPR.

GDPR has placed a huge responsibility on technology companies to ensure they take appropriate measures to be clear about what data is collected, how much data will be collected, how their data will be used, secure their users data from potential leaks and also fulfill the users rights to either access, review, or request deletion of their personal data.

3.1 Review the benefits and drawbacks of IT tools and systems used in terms of productivity and efficiency.

Could you have been more efficient when making your website? How? Could you have done it in teams?

IT tools and systems are essential to getting a group project to work as there is often many plates spinning simultaneously. They help plan, visualise, and discuss the progress of the project as well as reliably map it as opposed to using a single project manager with no involvement from the other team members. For group work, it is hard to envisage a successful project without them.

There are drawbacks to using these tools though. When it comes to solo projects, if the project creator already has a strong direction for the project, adding in additional tools can often end up hindering as they’re essentially adding admin for ideas which do not need to be communicated or referred to later.

Furthermore, when it comes to admin, there can be too much of a good thing. If there are superfluous tools, it can lead to a duplication of tracked information and ends up being a time sink instead of useful.

As the website was a solo project, additional project management tools were unnecessary as I had already knew how I wanted my website to look and would have found it difficult to build somebody else’s website as there would be a lot of back-and-forth to check both of us are seeing the same idea.

As the website is a very simple conception, working in a team would mean we could have more features and more idea to the website, although I am uncertain if that would necessarily be more efficient.


3.2 Describe ways to improve productivity and efficiency.

Productivity and efficiency are always at the forefront of project work as the time saved can often be spent elsewhere, making the project more successful overall. Additional time could be spent on identifying project ending problems or creating something which would be a huge quality of life increase to the user experience.

Efficiency and productivity go hand in hand and often being with thorough planning. By planning ahead into the future and aiming to complete a list of tasks within a certain time frame really influences progress. That progress over time should be tracked in some way so it can be visually represented and shown to other internal or external stakeholders. An additional benefit is tracked progress can be used as tool to highlight bottlenecks in the process and presents itself a compelling argument for additional resources.

Another technical aspect to productivity is being able to pivot away from problems, which are simply too time consuming to fix, or features which are take too long to implement, once new information has been obtained. Sometimes it’s better to give up on an already built losing strategy in favour of something which has the chance to work.

A less technical aspect worth mentioning is maintaining well-being. Having frequent, short breaks away from the workstation to get a drink and giving your eyes a rest from the monitor is a great way of feeling refreshed.

Lastly, encouraging good team work and communication between team members is something which is great for encouraging productivity. A team of individuals who are aiming to be the best performer are often outshone by a team of people who are willing to work together.

3.4 Describe how you would go about testing digital solutions

Once a project is past the concept stage and has been built, it has to be thoroughly tested in order to make sure it is able to work as intended.

Implementing a detailed error logging system is a great way of monitoring how well a product is handling various tasks whether it’s handling users connecting, data load, or even potential security breaches it’s always good to have a record of what hasn’t gone smoothly. With that comes an established method of handling these errors, recording the solution so they can be referred to should it happen again, and applying a, ideally, permanent fix to the problem.

Stress testing the products capabilities is something else which should be explored and something which small businesses and websites discover too late when they unexpectedly go viral. Knowing the limits of a product should be important so decisions can be made on whether to invest more into expanding the infrastructure or not well ahead of time.

From a non-technical point of view, gathering feedback from focus groups of potential users is also important to reinforce any decisions and to make sure the product remains on point to the desired audiences.

Describing copyright and other constraints affecting websites.

The online world allows for information to be shared freely, including written content such as text and visual content such as graphics. Whilst this is great in so many ways, the issue lies with it being easy for another person to steal another’s work and claim it as their own.

This really affects websites because copyrights are prevalent everywhere. Popular gaming streaming service Twitch.tv, owned by Amazon, had significant backlash from their community due to their exercise of the Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA). Despite streamers listening to a variety of music in the background being an almost ubiquitous occurrence, Twitch decided to mass delete all content which had any music playing in the background protected by the DMCA, resulting in thousands of streamers content being removed from Twitch’s website.

This is just one, prominent example about the importance of doing due diligence and appropriately crediting content creators featured in a live digital product.


1.6.2 Describing access issues that need taking into account - describe issues that people with disabilities may face when using webpages and how we can overcome them.

It’s important to consider accessibility when it comes to webpages as all webpages should be designed so they can be enjoyed by everybody and that design, in itself, comes with challenges which must be overcome.

With the web being a very visual and auditory experience, these would be two of the more challenging obstacles to address when it comes to web design. For those who are hard of hearing, a simple fix would be any media content to have subtitles or even an optional sign language speaker.

For those with a visual disability, making content which is optimised to be accessed by assistive screen reader technology by, for example, allowing text to be in place of images. For conditions such as colour vision deficiency, an optional mode to activate colours which cater to the user’s particular vision deficiency would be a great solution to implement at the start of the project.

Ultimately, as designers, it is up to us to maintain what the internet allows – inclusivity and access for all.


1.7.2 Describe which are the appropriate filetypes for websites.

There are many filetypes for the same type of media available to use whether it’s a JPG, an SVG, a PNG, an MP3, a WAV, an MP4, or an MPEG, the list goes on and on. But why should we care about the format?

The format matters because from a user experience perspective, nobody enjoys having a slow, unresponsive experience when using a website. So, the first port of call would be an image which can be easily loaded i.e small in size and static as opposed to an animated image which constantly loops (I’m a huge advocate of animated media only loading when you want it to).

Quality is also an important factor in this careful balancing act as it can sometimes lead to alienating users. Popular media streaming websites change based on the quality the user can support, actively switching to a lower quality if the higher resolutions are causing buffering.
